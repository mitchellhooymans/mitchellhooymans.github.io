{/* Educational Info */}

Dispersion vs. Rotation: Unlike spiral galaxies, globular clusters are typically dispersion-dominated. The stars do not rotate together; instead, they swarm in random elliptical orbits, supported against collapse by their random velocities.

Tidal Stripping & The Jacobi Radius: A cluster is bound by its own gravity ($M_c$), but the host galaxy ($M_g$) exerts a differential gravitational pull. The boundary where these forces balance is the Tidal Radius or Jacobi Radius ($r_t$):

{`$$r_t = R_{\\text{orb}} \\left( \\frac{M_c}{3 M_g} \\right)^{1/3}$$`}

Lagrange Points & Tidal Tails: When the cluster physically expands or moves too close to the galaxy ($r_h \approx r_t$), stars spill over the tidal boundary. They primarily escape through the inner and outer Lagrange points ($L_1$ and $L_2$), which lie along the axis connecting the cluster to the galaxy.

  • Leading Tail (Blue): Stars escaping from $L_1$ (towards the galaxy) fall into lower, faster orbits, pulling ahead of the cluster.
  • Trailing Tail (Red): Stars escaping from $L_2$ (away from the galaxy) enter higher, slower orbits, falling behind the cluster.
